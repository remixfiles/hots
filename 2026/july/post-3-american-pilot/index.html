Deadly Message from Papua: American Pilot Killed as Rebels Burn His Plane

HOTS
Editorial Team
July 7, 2026
7 min read

On July 2, 2026, separatist fighters from the West Papua National Liberation Army attacked an American civilian pilot and his aircraft at a remote airstrip in Indonesia’s Highland Papua province. Nicholas F. Gosselin, who had previously worked as a pilot in Alaska and was employed by the domestic airline PT AMA to deliver food, fuel, mail and other supplies to isolated villages, was shot dead shortly after landing his small aircraft at the Ipdeheik airstrip in Balinggama village, Yahukimo region. Seven local Papuan passengers on board escaped unharmed, but the plane was set on fire and destroyed. Communications with the aircraft were lost moments after touchdown, prompting an immediate investigation by Indonesian aviation authorities who confirmed no prior security alerts had been raised for the routine supply flight.

The armed group’s spokesperson, Sebby Sambom, stated that fighters opened fire and burned the aircraft because it had violated a standing ultimatum banning all civilian flights into areas under their control. The separatists claimed such planes were routinely used to transport Indonesian military personnel and logistics into contested zones, despite the operator’s stated purpose of serving civilian communities in one of the world’s most isolated regions. Sambom described the killing as a deliberate message to both the Indonesian and United States governments for failing to address the underlying causes of the decades-long conflict in Papua, and warned that further attacks would follow if civilian aviation continued to operate in designated “red zones.” The incident occurred amid heightened tensions in the resource-rich western half of New Guinea, where indigenous Papuan groups have pursued independence from Indonesia since the territory’s incorporation in 1969.

Indonesian security forces recovered Gosselin’s body on July 3 after weather conditions delayed access to the remote site, and it was subsequently transferred to the United States Embassy in Jakarta for repatriation to his family. The military confirmed the attack was carried out by the separatist group and announced an ongoing manhunt for those responsible, while emphasizing that all passengers had returned safely to their homes. The event has drawn renewed international attention to the protracted low-intensity conflict in Papua, which has seen repeated incidents involving foreign nationals, including the 2023 kidnapping and later release of a New Zealand pilot and the fatal shooting of another New Zealander in 2024. Indonesian authorities continue to investigate the circumstances of the flight and the broader security implications for civilian aviation serving remote communities, underscoring the challenges of operating in a region marked by longstanding separatist grievances and government counter-insurgency operations.
